Apps

With the long expected news of support for Kubernetes in Docker's community and commercial products (and, to a lesser extent, Oracle's hat-tip to Kubernetes at OpenWorld/JavaOne as well as Cloud Foundry's Kubo project) the conversation is now rapidly shifting from Kubernetes' relevance and future into the application and serverless platforms built on top of it. This brings us back to OpenShift, Cloud Foundry (yes, really) and the serverless players out there such as Fission, OpenWhisk, OpenFaaS and others. I've seen a few positions coming through: 1) that the orchestrator conversation is irrelevant because serverless is where is at, and 2) that efforts in Docker and Cloud Foundry spaces to bring in Kubernetes are signalling differentiation (for example, kubo vs. Diego as application vs. container runtime) I think the former is hyperbole (with juxtaposed positions 1 and 2) and the latter is an effort to rationalize the rise of Kubernetes, so don't get too hung up on either one. I tend to side with Krish on this one, and the discussion is a good read. The only thing that is clear to me is more tactical (almost a punchline): with less attention on "enterprise distributions", entrenched tooling (such as Docker's) using Kubernetes and developer's attention focused on platforms, kubectl is taking the backseat on how people understand and interact with Kubernetes. Finally, Open Service Broker API is one to watch. Josh's CF Summit Europe presentation is a must, but see this list for a comprehensive compilation. The takeaways:
  • When formulating your Kubernetes strategy, assess the platforms (incl. serverless) that can best leverage it rather than try to solve the orchestration problem
  • As soon as you are exposing an app to external customers and/or looking at public cloud, consider bringing Open Service Broker API into your architecture

Data platform

If open source and containers are changing the application platform at an unprecedented pace, the news in the data front are mindblowing. I grew up in an era where Postgres was seen as a way to reduce costs for Oracle shops and MySQL was what PHP developers used, and although Postgres had an edge, both products really sucked at things like clustering and high availability. Yet NoSQL in the form of Couch and then Mongo, showed us that the problem was less about elegant clusters and more about demolishing constraints and democratizing many use cases. Up until a few years ago the biggest open source data thing happening in my mind (besides open source-driven big data solutions) was Postgres' comeback, beating NoSQL solutions at many use cases, making powerful technology easy to use and not compromising on performance, security and technical quality. Postgres broke the mold, dare I say even from a demographics standpoint, and cloud vendors seem to like it. However, it's not a respectable month if there isn't at least one hnews thread where the discussion reflects what we can expect from data platform innovation in a cloud world. Other good reads highlighting innovation in this space are TiDB and YugaByte. A wild guess: we will see Postgres increasingly everywhere, replacing and/or taking over use cases most people don't think of. It will coexist with a few other specialized, highly innovative open source databases in some scenarios such as tech startups, but what I think is new is the potential for some of these solutions to permeate shadow IT.
